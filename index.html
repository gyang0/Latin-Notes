Declensions

What the heck are those

Declensions are for nouns. There are 5 declensions and 6 cases in Latin. Yippee.


How to recognize each declension

When in doubt, refer to word endings.

Nominative ending Genitive ending
1st declension -a -am
2nd declension -us/er -i
3rd declension [VARIES] -is
4th declension -us -us
5th declension -es -ei

More on those cases

Declensions + cases

Words in each declension take on different endings depending on the case they're in. Also, they change depending on whether the context calls for singular or plural. They keep the stem though.

Conjugations

Conjugations are for verbs. There are 4 different conjugations in Latin, depending on the verb stem endings (everything in Latin comes back to word endings).



1st conjugation: present stem ends in a

Example word: parāre (to prepare), present stem para/parā

Watch out for the subtle stem differences.

parō I prepare
parā-s you (sing.) prepare
para-t he/she prepares
parā-mus we prepare
parā-tis you (pl.) prepare
para-nt they prepare


2nd conjugation: present stem ends in e

Example word: monēre (to warn), present stem mone/monē

mone-ō I warn
monē-s you (sing.) warn
mone-t he/she warns
monē-mus we warn
monē-tis you (pl.) warn
mone-nt they warn


3rd conjugation: present stem ends in a consonant

Example word: regere (to rule), present stem reg

The endings here are a little different.

reg-ō I rule
reg-is you (sing.) rule
reg-it he/she rules
reg-imus we rule
reg-itis you (pl.) rule
reg-unt they rule


4th conjugation: present stem ends in i

Example word: audīre (to listen), present stem audi/audī

audi-ō I listen
audī-s you (sing.) listen
audi-t he/she listens
audī-mus we listen
audī-tis you (pl.) listen
audi-unt they listen

Tenses

Past, present, future, etc. For verbs.



Present

Everything in the "Conjugations" section is in the present tense.

Used for actions in the present (which is kinda obvious).



Imperfect

Used for actions in the past that are continuous or incomplete, like "I was going" or "you were playing."


The general structure of [present stem] + [verb ending] stays the same.

General form:

meaning ending
I was ____-ing -bam
you (sing.) were ____-ing -bās
he/she was ____-ing -bat
we were ____-ing -bāmus
you (pl.) were ____-ing -bātis
they were ____-ing -bant

But for the 3rd and 4th conjugation verbs (that is, verbs whose stems end in a consonant or an i), an extra ē is added.


Example words (note the difference between the present tense in the stem's macrons - the imperfect tense is way more consistent.)

1st conjugation 2nd conjugation 3rd conjugation 4th conjugation
I was ___-ing parā-bam monē-bam reg-ē-bam audi-ē-bam
you (sing.) were ___-ing parā-bās monē-bās reg-ē-bās audi-ē-bās
he/she was ___-ing parā-bat monē-bat reg-ē-bat audi-ē-bat
we were ___-ing parā-bāmus monē-bāmus reg-ē-bāmus audi-ē-bāmus
you (pl.) were ___-ing parā-bātis monē-bātis reg-ē-bātis audi-ē-bātis
they were ___-ing parā-bant monē-bant reg-ē-bant audi-ē-bant


Perfect

Used for an action in the past that's been completed. This is the difference between the perfect and the imperfect.


The good news is that the verb endings don't have any particular exceptions. They're all pretty consistent across the 4 conjugations. The bad news is that we have to memorize another type of stem, the perfect stem. So far we've only used the present stem.


Some examples of the perfect stem:

word present stem perfect stem
parāre (to prepare) para/parā parāu
monēre (to warn) mone/monē monu
regere (to rule) reg rēx
audīre (to listen) audi/audī audīu

Note the similarities between the present stem and the perfect stem. Except for the 3rd conjugation, the perfect stems seems to be

[present stem] + [u], give or take a few letters.


Perfect tense endings:

meaning ending (attached to perfect stem)
I ____-ed -ī
you (sing.) ____-ed -istī
he/she ____-ed -it
we ____-ed -imus
you (pl.) ____-ed -istis
they ____-ed -ērunt


Pluperfect

Used for words that represent an action in the past which comes before another action in the past. It sounds confusing written like that, but the syntax is pretty natural: stuff like "I had baked" or "you had gone."


The endings here are pretty regular too, so yay for that.

meaning ending
I had ____-ed -eram
you (sing.) had ____-ed -erās
he/she had ____-ed -erat
we had ____-ed -erāmus
you (pl.) had ____-ed -erātis
they had ____-ed -erant

Reflexive pronouns

Reflexive pronouns are used when an action refers back to the subject. It's probably easier to see this in examples.

Latin English
ego mē lauō I wash myself
tū tē lauās you (sing.) wash yourself
ille sē lauat he washes himself
nōs nōs lauāmus we wash ourselves
uōs uōs lauātis you (pl.) wash yourselves
illī sē lauant they wash themselves

The personal adjectives:

meus/mea/meum my
tuus/tua/tuum your (sing.)
suus/sua/suum his/her own
notser/nostra/nostrum our
uester/uestra/uestrum your (pl.)
suus/sua/suum their own

And some examples pulled from the book:

  • Crassus cupit suam glōriam augēre. - Crassus wants to increase his own glory
  • pueri suōs loculōs ferunt. - The boys are carrying their own satchels.
  • pueri ad ludum festīnant, paedagōgī loculōs eōrum ferunt. - The boys are hurrying to school; the tutors are carrying their (the boys') satchels

As seen in the last example, Latin uses suus to refer back to the subject, but eius (from the is pronoun) to refer to something other than the subject.
